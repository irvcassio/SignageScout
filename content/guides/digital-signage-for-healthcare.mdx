Healthcare facilities use digital signage for patient wayfinding, waiting room information, staff communication, and emergency alerts. This guide covers what healthcare IT teams need to know when deploying digital signage in a clinical environment.

## Key Use Cases in Healthcare

### Patient Wayfinding

Large hospitals are notoriously difficult to navigate. Interactive kiosk displays at entrances and elevator lobbies help patients find departments, clinics, and services. [TouchSource](/vendors/touchsource) specializes in wayfinding kiosks for healthcare facilities, with ADA-compliant touch interfaces and multi-language support.

### Waiting Room Displays

Screens in waiting rooms show estimated wait times, health education content, appointment queue status, and facility announcements. This reduces perceived wait times and improves patient satisfaction scores.

### Staff Communication

Digital displays in break rooms, nursing stations, and corridors show shift schedules, safety alerts, quality metrics, and recognition programs. [Poppulo](/vendors/poppulo) (formerly Four Winds Interactive) is widely used for internal healthcare communications.

### Emergency Alerts

Digital signage integrates with hospital emergency notification systems to display code alerts, evacuation routes, and lockdown instructions. Screens override normal content instantly when an alert triggers.

### Donor Recognition

Hospital lobbies use digital donor walls to recognize contributors. Dynamic displays can rotate multiple donors, show campaign progress, and update without physical plaque changes.

## Compliance Considerations

### HIPAA

Digital signage in healthcare must never display protected health information (PHI) on public-facing screens. Patient names on queue boards must be handled carefully — many facilities use number-based systems or first-name-only displays.

**Safe patterns:**
- Queue number systems instead of patient names
- Anonymous wait time estimates
- General health education content on public screens
- Staff-only displays behind secure areas for schedule details

### ADA Accessibility

Interactive kiosks must comply with ADA requirements:
- Screen height accessible from a wheelchair (48 inches max to top of interactive area)
- High contrast text and large fonts
- Audio output option for visually impaired users
- Physical buttons or accessible touch targets

### Network Security

Hospital IT networks are segmented for security. Digital signage should run on a dedicated VLAN, separate from clinical systems. Media players should not have access to clinical networks or patient data systems.

## Recommended Hardware

Healthcare environments need commercial-grade displays rated for extended operation:

- **Waiting rooms:** [Samsung](/vendors/samsung-business) QBR/QMR series (500 nits, 16/7) or [LG](/vendors/lg-business) UH5N series
- **Wayfinding kiosks:** Interactive touch displays in freestanding enclosures from [Legrand AV](/vendors/legrand-av) (Chief kiosk line)
- **Nursing stations:** Compact displays with [BrightSign](/vendors/brightsign) LS5 players for simple content
- **Lobbies:** Video walls from [Planar](/vendors/planar) for donor recognition and facility branding
